# Spec: extract named places and infra-ordinary spatial entities

## Motivation

The current extraction prompt is designed mainly for conventional literary place indexing: it asks the LLM to find explicit real-world place mentions such as cities, streets, countries, districts, landmarks, buildings, rivers, etc.

For Georges Perec, this is useful but incomplete. Perec's work is strongly concerned with ordinary, domestic, repeated, and infra-ordinary spatial forms: rooms, beds, staircases, cafés, streets, courtyards, buildings, windows, tables, buses, métro stations, neighborhoods, and other everyday spatial supports.

A strict named-entity approach risks treating these mentions as noise, even though they may be central to a spatial reading of Perec.

The goal of this change is therefore not to replace named-place extraction, but to distinguish two complementary layers:

1. **Named / identifiable places**: proper toponyms and named geographical entities.
2. **Infra-ordinary spatial entities**: generic but spatially meaningful nouns and expressions.

This makes it possible to build both:

- a conventional place index;
- a typology of ordinary spaces in Perec's texts.

## Conceptual distinction

### 1. `named_place`

A `named_place` is an explicit proper-name place mention. It may be real-world, fictional, uncertain, or constructed within the work. The extraction step should not try to settle that referential question; it should simply preserve the spatial name for later human or gazetteer review.

Examples of named places:

- `Paris`
- `rue Vilin`
- `place Saint-Sulpice`
- `avenue Junot`
- `Mabillon`
- `Ellis Island`
- `Londres`
- `Tunisie`
- `la Seine`
- `Montparnasse`

Important fictional or constructed example:

- `rue Simon-Crubellier`

This is especially important for *La Vie mode d'emploi*: constructed named places such as `rue Simon-Crubellier` should be extracted, not excluded because they may not be geocodable.

Named places may receive geographic metadata when confident, but only when directly supported by the text or very common knowledge: `city`, `region`, `country`.

### 2. `generic_spatial_entity`

A `generic_spatial_entity` is not necessarily a named place, but it denotes a spatial object, setting, container, route, threshold, dwelling, infrastructure, or ordinary spatial form.

Examples:

- `la chambre`
- `le lit`
- `la table`
- `l'immeuble`
- `l'escalier`
- `le palier`
- `la cour`
- `la rue`
- `le quartier`
- `le café`
- `la cuisine`
- `le bureau`
- `la cave`
- `le grenier`
- `la fenêtre`
- `la porte`
- `le mur`
- `la maison`
- `le passage`
- `le trottoir`
- `la ville`
- `la campagne`
- `le monde`
- `l'espace`
- `la banlieue`
- `la zone libre`
- `le métro`
- `l'autobus`
- `le train`
- `le pays`

These should normally **not** receive geographic metadata unless the text explicitly links them to a named place.

For example:

- `la chambre` → generic spatial entity, no city/country
- `la rue Vilin` → named place, city = Paris, country = France
- `la rue` → generic spatial entity, no city/country
- `une rue de Paris` → generic spatial entity with city = Paris if useful, but not a named street

## Proposed schema change

The current schema uses:

```json
{
  "place_text": "string",
  "normalized_place": "string",
  "place_type": "city | street | district | landmark | building | country | region | river | natural_feature | other | null",
  "city": "string or null",
  "region": "string or null",
  "country": "string or null",
  "confidence": 0.0,
  "context": "string"
}
```

Recommended new schema:

```json
{
  "mentions": [
    {
      "mention_text": "string",
      "normalized_text": "string",
      "mention_kind": "named_place | generic_spatial_entity",
      "spatial_type": "city | street | square | district | country | region | water | natural_feature | building | institution | room | domestic_space | threshold | circulation | public_space | commercial_space | work_space | transport_space | vehicle | micro_place | other",
      "usage": "literal | metaphorical | uncertain",
      "city": "string or null",
      "region": "string or null",
      "country": "string or null",
      "confidence": 0.0,
      "context": "string"
    }
  ]
}
```

Notes:

- `mention_text` replaces `place_text`, because not all extracted entities are places in the strict toponymic sense.
- `normalized_text` replaces `normalized_place` for the same reason.
- `mention_kind` is the key analytical distinction.
- `spatial_type` gives a more detailed typology.
- `usage` helps distinguish literal spatial description from metaphorical or conceptual uses.
- Do not include `referential_status`. In literary works, the real/fictional/constructed status of a place is often interpretive and should be left to later human or gazetteer review.
- Do not include a separate `is_named` field. It is redundant and can be derived downstream from `mention_kind == "named_place"`, avoiding contradictory model output.

## Normalization rules

### For named places

- Preserve the exact surface form in `mention_text`.
- Use the usual French or local canonical form in `normalized_text`.
- Do **not** translate French place names into English.

Examples:

- `Grèce` → `Grèce`, not `Greece`
- `Londres` → `Londres`, not `London`
- `États-Unis` → `États-Unis`, not `United States`
- `Corne d'Or` → `Corne d'Or`, not `Golden Horn`
- `la rue Vilin` → `rue Vilin`
- `la rue Simon-Crubellier` → `rue Simon-Crubellier`

### For generic spatial entities

- Preserve the French term.
- Normalize only lightly: remove articles when useful, use singular lemma when obvious, but do not over-normalize.
- Do not turn generic spatial nouns into named places.
- Extract generic nouns or noun phrases when they function as spatial settings, containers, routes, thresholds, infrastructures, or supports in the passage.
- Do not extract ordinary objects merely because they are physically located somewhere.
- Do not extract living beings or body parts as spatial entities.
- Exclude food, materials, decorative objects, tools, documents, artworks, machines, signs, posters, small movable objects, and incidental props, unless they explicitly designate a place.
- Use `micro_place` only for small-scale supports where a body or activity can settle: beds, tables, desks, chairs, armchairs, bookcases/libraries. This project is interested in Perec's infra-ordinary spaces, not in ordinary objects as such.
- Use `spatial_type = other` rarely. Do not use `other` as a fallback for ordinary objects.

Examples:

- `la chambre` → `chambre`
- `les chambres` → `chambre`
- `l'immeuble` → `immeuble`
- `les escaliers` → `escalier`
- `la ville` → `ville`, not a specific city
- `le pays` → `pays`, not a specific country

## Prompt draft

```text
Extract spatial mentions from the French literary text below.

Return only valid JSON matching this schema:
{
  "mentions": [
    {
      "mention_text": "string",
      "normalized_text": "string",
      "mention_kind": "named_place | generic_spatial_entity",
      "spatial_type": "city | street | square | district | country | region | water | natural_feature | building | institution | room | domestic_space | threshold | circulation | public_space | commercial_space | work_space | transport_space | vehicle | micro_place | other",
      "usage": "literal | metaphorical | uncertain",
      "city": "string or null",
      "region": "string or null",
      "country": "string or null",
      "confidence": number,
      "context": "string"
    }
  ]
}

Definitions:

1. named_place
Extract explicit named places: cities, countries, regions, streets, squares, districts, landmarks, buildings, institutions used as places, rivers, islands, seas, mountains, stations, parks, etc.

Named places may be real-world, fictional, uncertain, or constructed within the work. This is important for Perec: extract `rue Simon-Crubellier` and similar constructed named places. Do not omit them because they may not be geocodable. Do not try to decide definitively whether named places are real or fictional.

Examples: Paris, rue Vilin, place Saint-Sulpice, avenue Junot, Mabillon, Ellis Island, Londres, Tunisie, la Seine, Montparnasse, rue Simon-Crubellier.

2. generic_spatial_entity
Also extract generic but spatially meaningful entities, especially those relevant to Georges Perec's attention to ordinary and infra-ordinary space.

Examples: chambre, lit, table, appartement, immeuble, escalier, palier, cour, rue, quartier, café, cuisine, bureau, cave, grenier, fenêtre, porte, mur, maison, passage, trottoir, ville, campagne, monde, espace, banlieue, zone libre, métro, autobus, train, pays.

Use `micro_place` only for small-scale supports where a body or activity can settle: lit, table, bureau, chaise, fauteuil, bibliothèque. This project is interested in Perec's infra-ordinary spaces, not in ordinary objects as such.

Rules:

- Extract only mentions explicitly present in the text.
- Preserve the exact surface form in mention_text.
- Use normalized_text for a conservative French/local canonical form.
- All normalized_text values should be in French or in the local original form.
- Never translate French place names into English.
- Do not try to decide definitively whether a named place is real or fictional.
- Do not invent geographic metadata.
- Fill city, region, and country only when directly supported by the text or very common knowledge.
- For Paris streets, squares, métro stations, monuments, and districts, city = Paris and country = France when confident.
- For generic_spatial_entity, usually leave city, region, and country null unless the text explicitly links the entity to a named place.
- For generic_spatial_entity, extract nouns or noun phrases that function as spatial settings, containers, routes, thresholds, infrastructures, broad spatial categories, or stable micro-spatial supports in the passage.
- Do not extract ordinary objects merely because they are physically located somewhere.
- For micro_place, extract only these small-scale supports where a body or activity can settle: lit, table, bureau, chaise, fauteuil, bibliothèque.
- Do not extract other furniture such as armoire, placard, étagère, commode, buffet, vaisselier, canapé, miroir, lavabo, or baignoire.
- Exclude food, materials, decorative objects, tools, documents, artworks, machines, signs, posters, small movable objects, storage furniture, and incidental props, unless they explicitly designate a place.
- Do not extract living beings or body parts as spatial entities.
- Use spatial_type = other rarely. Do not use other as a fallback for ordinary objects.
- Set usage = literal for concrete spatial description, metaphorical for figurative or conceptual spatial uses, and uncertain when the distinction is unclear.
- Exclude people, publishers, collection titles, book titles, section titles, and organizations unless the wording clearly refers to a physical place.
- Ignore tables of contents and front matter such as title pages, copyright pages, publishing-house addresses, collection catalogues, ISBN pages, and lists of works. Do not extract spatial-looking section titles from a table of contents.
- Exclude purely abstract uses when there is no spatial meaning.
- Extract each distinct mention occurrence in the passage, not only one example per type. If the same expression appears repeatedly in different sentences, include each occurrence with its own context. If it is repeated several times in the same short sentence or list, one mention is sufficient.
- Include a short context sentence or phrase.
- Return an empty mentions array if no spatial mentions are found.

Confidence scale:

- 0.95: exact, explicit, unambiguous mention; normalization certain.
- 0.80: clear spatial mention; minor uncertainty about type or metadata.
- 0.60: spatial mention is plausible but ambiguous or context-dependent.
- 0.40: uncertain; normally omit unless the mention is analytically important.

Text:
{chunk.text}
```

## Suggested system prompt

```text
You extract spatial mentions from French literary texts for a scholarly project on Georges Perec. Return only valid JSON. Distinguish named places from generic infra-ordinary spatial entities, and distinguish literal from metaphorical usage. Be conservative with geographic metadata. Keep normalized names in French or local original form; never translate French place names into English.
```

## Output strategy

This change does **not** need to preserve compatibility with the existing `place_mentions.csv` schema. The project is exploratory and under version control; older outputs and schemas can be recovered from git history if needed.

Prefer the cleanest schema for the research question rather than a backward-compatible adaptation of the first version.

The old columns should be replaced:

```text
place_text
normalized_place
place_type
```

with the more general spatial-mention columns:

```text
mention_text
normalized_text
mention_kind
spatial_type
usage
```

Rationale:

- `place_text` and `normalized_place` imply that every extracted item is a place in the conventional toponymic sense.
- Perec's infra-ordinary spatial entities are often not named places, but they are still spatially meaningful.
- A clean schema avoids treating generic spatial entities as malformed or noisy place mentions.

## Recommended CSV outputs

The project should produce three complementary files:

```text
output/spatial_mentions.csv
output/named_place_index.csv
output/spatial_type_index.csv
```

Where:

- `spatial_mentions.csv` contains all extracted mentions, both named places and generic spatial entities.
- `named_place_index.csv` contains only `mention_kind = named_place`, grouped by `normalized_text`, `city`, `region`, and `country`.
- `spatial_type_index.csv` aggregates all mentions by work, `mention_kind`, `spatial_type`, and `normalized_text`.

The old `place_mentions.csv` / `place_index.csv` names can be retired or regenerated later if a conventional toponym-only export is needed.

## Evaluation questions

After a test run, review whether the new prompt captures:

- ordinary domestic spaces: room, bed, table, kitchen, apartment;
- building morphology: staircase, landing, corridor, courtyard, cellar, attic;
- public ordinary spaces: street, square, café, shop, métro, bus;
- named Parisian places: streets, squares, stations, districts;
- fictional or constructed named places, especially `rue Simon-Crubellier` in *La Vie mode d'emploi*;
- cases where generic and named space interact, e.g. `une rue de Paris`, `l'immeuble de la rue Vilin`.

Also check for over-generation:

- purely metaphorical uses of `place`, `espace`, `monde` incorrectly marked as literal;
- non-spatial institutions;
- paratextual collection titles, book titles, section titles, table-of-contents entries, or publishing-house addresses wrongly treated as physical places;
- adjectives and demonyms;
- living beings, body parts, food, materials, decorative objects, tools, documents, artworks, machines, signs, posters, small movable objects, or incidental props extracted as spatial entities;
- generic nouns in irrelevant contexts;
- micro_place extracted outside the narrow list: lit, table, bureau, chaise, fauteuil, bibliothèque.

## Rationale

This change explicitly treats some traditional NER "noise" as potential literary signal. That is especially appropriate for Perec, whose writing repeatedly attends to ordinary spatial forms and everyday arrangements. The important methodological point is not to confuse these mentions with geocodable named places. The extraction should keep them visible, but analytically separate.
